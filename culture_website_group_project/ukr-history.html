History of Ukraine

Long ago, the territory that is now Ukraine was inhabited by various peoples. Ancient Slavs, Scythians, and Goths populated these lands. However, the true history of Ukraine began to take shape in the 9th century with the emergence of Kyivan Rus. Kyiv became its capital, a center of trade, culture, and Christianity.

In the year 988, Kyivan Rus adopted Christianity, which had a profound influence on the formation of culture and society. It became the first Slavic state with a Christian faith.

In the subsequent centuries, Ukraine found itself within the sphere of influence of various states, including Poland and Lithuania. This led to the formation of two regional parts â€“ Western and Eastern Ukraine. In the 17th century, during the uprising led by Khmelnytsky, Ukrainians sought liberation from Polish influence.

Those were the days of independence and cultural development until the year 1922 when Ukraine became part of the Soviet Union. But before that moment, Ukraine had its own path, rich in history and influenced by various cultures.

During the Soviet era, Ukraine became part of the Soviet Union in 1922, becoming one of its constituent republics. This period was marked by economic changes, industrialization, and collectivization, which proved challenging for the population. In the course of the Second World War, Ukraine experienced tragic events, such as the Holodomor (famine-genocide) and destruction related to military actions.

During this period, Ukraine became a hub for underground organizations such as the Ukrainian Insurgent Army (UPA), which fought for Ukraine's independence from Soviet rule. The insurgent movement was an expression of national protest and the desire for freedom. Nevertheless, Soviet authorities brutally suppressed these attempts and subjected participants to repression.

Despite the suppression, the spirit of national resistance persisted. Ultimately, in 1991, Ukraine declared its independence after the collapse of the Soviet Union. This period was a crucial moment in the country's history, where national aspirations and the feats of insurgent movements influenced the formation of Ukrainian independence.

Since gaining independence in 1991, Ukraine has embarked on a path of rapid change, focused on strengthening democracy, developing civil society, and integrating into the European community. In the early 2000s, the country faced challenges in consolidating its democratic institutions, leading to significant reforms across various societal spheres.

A pivotal moment was the "Orange Revolution" in 2004, where mass protests resulted in a reevaluation of presidential election results and the active promotion of democratic principles. This period profoundly influenced the political culture of the country.

Ukraine's aspiration to join the European community became a catalyst for a series of changes in 2014 during the Euromaidan protests. These events led to a change of power and a vibrant expression of the nation's desire for integration into the European family. In 2017, Ukraine signed the Association Agreement with the European Union, signaling its strong commitment to moving toward European standards.

Today, Ukraine, more than ever, is fighting for its independence, interests, and freedom, leaving behind memories of the Soviet era and forging new relationships with Europe.
